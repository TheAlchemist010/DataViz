Australia's Renewable Transition

1970: The Rise of Enviromentalism

Throughout the 1970s, Australia began to see a rise in enviromental concern by the general public. 1970 marked the first global Earth Day, and the mid-70s held some of the first major enviromental activism. Interestingly, much of this activism was focused towards damming projects, such as Lake Pedder and the Franklin Dam. The 1970s also included the foundational reasearch into the greenhouse effect and polution, including the observation that the globe was warming. The rate seemed very minor in the 70s, but today the climate is rapidly heating.

2015: The Paris Agreement

In 2015, representatives of 197 parties assembled in Paris to decide on the global respose to the global climate. Australia commit to a 26-28% reduction emmisions from the 2005 levels. Currently, 194 nations have signed the agreement, representing over 98% of emissions. Though Australian emissions have peaked and are declining, we are actually above the 2005 levels. That being said, the per-capita C02 emissions have decreased significantly since 2005.

2024 and the future

Though most of Australia's power is currently coming from fossil fuels, the vast majority of upcoming and in-construction projects are renewables. There are, however, still some fossil fuel stations being constructed, such as the Reeves Plains Power Station in South Australia that will involve six gas turbines to produce up to 300 megawatts.
